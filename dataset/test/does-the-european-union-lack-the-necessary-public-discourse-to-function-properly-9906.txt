Discussion Name: Does the European Union lack the necessary public discourse to function properly?

1. The European Union lacks the necessary public discourse to function properly.
1.1. Con: The only institutions that are able to create new legislation are the commissions who meet in private.
1.1.1. Con: The biggest issue is that the European Parliament cannot suggest new laws but can only vote on them.
1.1.2. Con: This makes the legislative process vulnerable to lobbying.
1.1.3. Pro: The more important commission meetings should be made accessible to the public
1.2. Pro: The lack of European level parties impairs the implementation of public discourse.
1.2.1. Con: The majority of major political parties in Europe have aligned themselves with one of [these](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_of_political_parties_in_Europe_by_pancontinental_organisation) pan-European political organizations.
1.2.2. Pro: There are multiple pan-European or transnational political movements that have organized themselves into political parties.
1.2.2.1. Pro: The [European Federalist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Federalist_Party)(The European Federalist Party is made up of members from 12 European countries, from civil society, who face their professional life and political commitment.) is working towards making the EU a true federalist state.
1.2.2.2. Pro: The pro-European federalist organization [Volt](https://www.volteuropa.org/about)(Our goal is to get our own political faction in the European Parliament in 2024. Volt competed in the European Elections in 2019 for the first time and we led a cross-border election campaign with a European election programme. As a result, Damian Boeselager became the first Volter in the European Parliament.) is a registered political party in multiple states.
1.2.2.3. Pro: [ALDE](https://www.aldeparty.eu/about_alde), the Alliance of Liberals and Democrats for Europe Party is a political party with multiple representatives in the EU parliament, as well as EU commissioners that identify with ALDE.
1.3. Pro: National politicians tend to blame the EU for their own failings instead of engaging in meaningful debates about reform on an EU level.
1.3.1. Pro: Politicians in the [UK](https://www.independent.co.uk/voices/letters/politicians-used-the-eu-as-a-scapegoat-for-forty-years-a7658686.html) have used the EU as a scapegoat for 40 years.
1.3.2. Pro: [Werner Hoyer](https://www.spiegel.de/international/europe/a-danger-of-backsliding-politicians-like-to-use-the-eu-as-a-scapegoat-a-773585.html), a senior German Foreign Ministry official, said that politicians 'like to use the EU as a scapegoat'.
1.4. Pro: In contrast to national public debates, there are no such things as EU newspapers or TV channels.
1.4.1. Con: Most news sites and newspapers have a section dedicated to reporting on EU politics and ongoing debate.
1.4.2. Pro: If debates were made public and reported, the population would become engaged. We need an unbiased Europe wide media channel.
1.4.3. Con: Multi-language institutions like arte.tv do support public debates in the EU.
1.4.3.1. Pro: Arte works closely with European institutions. Its[headquarters](http://www.telesatellite.com/infos/idisp.php?i=1158), for example, is part of the European Quarter in Strasbourg.
1.5. Pro: National interests are far too often used as arguments to undermine the EU's political process.
1.5.1. Pro: -> See 1.3.
1.6. Con: The EU is in essence a Union of national states, therefore the national debates must suffice to support the EU institutions.
1.7. Con: It is impossible to have public debates in the EU, because the language-barrier stands in the way.
1.7.1. Con: Within the EU, public discourse is possible. Anyone can start a platform, exchange ideas, even apply for EU funding for projects.
1.7.2. Con: Numerous citizens and representatives in the EU are proficient in multiple languages, making a debate possible. Debates can also easily be translated in any of the member states language making a debate possible to be broadcast on different media.
1.8. Con: The variety of people and political perspective makes it impossible for Europe to have actual public debates.
1.8.1. Con: In the United States, public debates are possible despite a wide variety of population groups and political affiliations. This suggests the same must be possible within the EU as well.
1.8.2. Con: The variety of voices in the union might make reaching a consensus decision harder, but ultimately such a decision is stronger than a national one.
