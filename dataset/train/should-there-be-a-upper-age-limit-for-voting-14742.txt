Discussion Name: Should there be a upper age limit for voting?

1. There should be an upper age limit for voting.
1.1. Pro: If the elderly are allowed to vote they would be interfering with younger generations and their ability to take responsibility for their own futures.
1.1.1. Con: Allowing the elderly to vote doesn't take away the ability of younger people to vote and their ability to take responsibility for their own futures.
1.1.1.1. Pro: In the US, it is shown that if young voters improved their [turnout rate](https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/monkey-cage/wp/2019/02/11/yes-young-people-voted-at-higher-rates-in-2018-but-so-did-every-age-group/), the influence of older voters in deciding governments is diminished.
1.1.1.1.1. Pro: In the [2018 US presidential elections](https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/monkey-cage/wp/2019/02/11/yes-young-people-voted-at-higher-rates-in-2018-but-so-did-every-age-group/), Democratic candidates would have done much better if a larger proportion of the young electorate would have voted.
1.1.2. Pro: In some recent [elections](https://metro.co.uk/2019/12/17/election-results-reveal-massive-divide-old-young-voters-11920781/), the political choices of the elderly and the young have been ideologically opposite.
1.1.2.1. Con: All groups with opposing or competing interests are likely to interfere with the interests of one another. Thus, this argument is not specific enough to explain why the elderly should be prevented from voting.
1.1.2.1.1. Con: One group, which is the young, is more likely to live with the consequences of contemporary political choices. Therefore, opposite interests can interfere with the lives of the young for longer.
1.1.2.1.1.1. Pro: Brexit is a political decision that is hard to reverse. The elderly in the UK supported it [disproportionately more than the young](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/brexit/2016/11/22/every-generation-votes-in-their-own-interest-but-in-an-ageing-world-thats-a-problem/) who will have to live with this irreversible political decision.
1.1.2.2. Pro: In some countries, [young people](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0016328716300520) are disproportionately smaller in number. This can mean that if there is a conflict between their interests and the interests of the elderly, the interests of the elderly are likely to be catered to.
1.1.2.2.1. Con: Equivalency among interests can be achieved by assigning different [weightage to votes](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0016328716300520) of different groups, without taking away the right to vote.
1.1.2.2.2. Pro: The [decreasing number of younger voters](http://eprints.lse.ac.uk/48304/1/blogs.lse.ac.uk-Cohort_size_matters_democracy_is_in_danger_as_young_peoples_disenfranchisement_accelerates%20%281%29.pdf) is bad for democracy because government policies will likely impact the lives of those currently young for a longer period of time.
1.1.2.2.2.1. Con: Citizens belonging to all demographic groups have the right to vote. The [disenfranchisement](http://eprints.lse.ac.uk/48304/1/blogs.lse.ac.uk-Cohort_size_matters_democracy_is_in_danger_as_young_peoples_disenfranchisement_accelerates%20%281%29.pdf) of one social group does not justify the disenfranchisement of another.
1.1.2.3. Pro: Older voters are generally [less supportive of government funding](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/brexit/2016/11/22/every-generation-votes-in-their-own-interest-but-in-an-ageing-world-thats-a-problem/) of projects that benefit younger generations in the long run.
1.1.2.3.1. Pro: During a [German referendum](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/brexit/2016/11/22/every-generation-votes-in-their-own-interest-but-in-an-ageing-world-thats-a-problem/), support for a massive transport project that would improve regional connectivity and free spaces for urbanization was significantly less among older voters.
1.1.2.4. Pro: In the UK, the elderly were more likely to support [Brexit](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/brexit/2016/11/22/every-generation-votes-in-their-own-interest-but-in-an-ageing-world-thats-a-problem/) than the young.
1.1.2.4.1. Con: Support for Brexit among the elderly should be seen as rooted in genuine concerns around [economic issues such as pensions.](https://www.independent.co.uk/news/uk/politics/brexit-why-did-old-people-vote-leave-young-voters-remain-eu-referendum-a7103996.html) These are legitimate concerns that should base people's electoral decisions.
1.1.2.4.2. Con: A [survey](http://www.bsa.natcen.ac.uk/media/39149/bsa34_brexit_final.pdf) found that education was a better predictor of preference for Brexit than age. The proportion of young people with a degree who voted remain was only 10% more than old people with a degree.
1.1.2.4.3. Con: Voting in a referendum is a genuine expression of political view in a democracy and should not result in disenfranchisement.
1.1.2.4.3.1. Pro: [Men are more likely](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2879390) to have voted in favor of Brexit than women, but this wouldn't entail their electoral disenfranchisement.
1.1.3. Pro: Political parties are increasingly focused on the [demands of older voters](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/17531055.2017.1410759) among other segments of the electorate.
1.1.3.1. Con: Voter participation among the elderly is [higher than among the young](http://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-856x.2006.00243.x). Since their votes play a bigger role in electing the government, their interests are naturally considered dominant.
1.1.3.1.1. Pro: If younger people would like parties to take their interests more seriously, they should vote in larger numbers.
1.1.3.2. Con: Practices such as [gerrymandering](https://www.americanprogress.org/issues/democracy/reports/2020/07/08/487426/partisan-gerrymandering-limits-voting-rights/) demonstrate that in US politics, it is the benefit of the voter to the party, not the parties benefit to the voter which is the motivation for a party to target a demographic such as the elderly.
1.1.3.3. Pro: Basing important policies upon the opinion of the elderly can virtually result in a state of the [gerontocracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerontocracy), which is a form of oligarchical rule.
1.1.3.4. Con: At the level of macroeconomic policy, the discourse has been gradually shifting away from providing [welfare](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/B9780120883882500225) to the elderly.
1.1.3.5. Con: Increasing focus on the demands of the elderly generates public good because it forces the government to increase public spending for [pension and health systems](https://www.imf.org/external/np/g20/pdf/2019/060519a.pdf). \(pg 1\)
1.1.3.5.1. Pro: The demands of [pensioners](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/08959420902955917) were instrumental in setting up the modern welfare state system in the West.
1.1.3.5.2. Con: Healthcare would be important for governments independent of the specific importance of healthcare to the elderly as it is valued by people of all ages.
1.1.3.6. Con: It is a good thing when one social group's electoral participation results in attracting higher political attention for the interests of that group. This restores faith in the idea that citizen groups can have political clout through voting.
1.1.3.6.1. Con: If politics is seen to increasingly pander to the interests of the elderly instead of the interests of the young, the [young will increasingly lose faith in electoral participation](https://www.dailymail.co.uk/news/article-8857377/Study-shows-millennials-losing-faith-democracy.html).
1.1.3.6.1.1. Pro: Today's young are more likely to remain as citizens of democratic countries for a long time to come. Their disenfranchisement poses an existential threat to [electoral democracy](http://eprints.lse.ac.uk/48304/1/blogs.lse.ac.uk-Cohort_size_matters_democracy_is_in_danger_as_young_peoples_disenfranchisement_accelerates%20%281%29.pdf).
1.1.3.7. Con: The implied claim here is that the elderly should not be allowed to vote as often \(age limit\) because they vote more often \(by choice\). The implication is that we should somehow remove voters from every identifiable cohort that votes more often, i.e. suppress voter turnout.
1.1.4. Con: The prospect of death is inevitable for all people and one's proximity to it should not determine the value of one's vote.
1.1.4.1. Pro: If all people are going to die, it is inconsistent to cite this as a reason for some groups not being allowed to vote.
1.1.4.2. Pro: It is not possible to predict exactly when one is going to die. Thus it is not possible to accurately determine one's proximity towards death.
1.1.5. Con: [Authoritarian populist governments](https://www.un.org/development/desa/dspd/wp-content/uploads/sites/22/2018/09/Norris.pdf) are often elected by the elderly vote. Such governments are likely to negatively impact democratic institutions which impact the political lives of the young.
1.1.5.1. Pro: In the US, [younger voters favor liberal policies](https://www.washingtonpost.com/news/monkey-cage/wp/2019/02/11/yes-young-people-voted-at-higher-rates-in-2018-but-so-did-every-age-group/) while the elderly favor conservative policies. If governments are consistently decided by the elderly, the political character of the government is likely to be conservative, contrary to what young voters want.
1.1.5.1.1. Con: If the liberal policies are not sufficient to motivate young voters to actually vote, it could be argued that those liberal policies are not as desirable as claimed.
1.1.5.2. Pro: In the UK, the elderly voted significantly more for Brexit than the young. The negative impacts of [Brexit](https://www.bbc.co.uk/bbcthree/article/b8d097b0-3ad4-4dd9-aa25-af6374292de0), however, will disproportionately impact the young in the UK.
1.2. Con: The elderly have [specific social and political needs](http://www.vikerkaar.ee/archives/26531?fbclid=IwAR3Lq0Vzk2anrP8ku2fB8-TIYhG3qYq0SpCQ1BiUIerFtZdXiCZiHyMErS8), which need political representation through electoral participation.
1.2.1. Pro: Elders make up a significant portion of the voters and can therefore have a lot of influence on the outcome of an election.
1.2.2. Con: Voting participation among the elderly is higher than among the young. This has sometimes led to their interests being given undue precedence over the [interests of young people](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2019/may/11/britain-favours-old-over-young-too-long-inequality-poverty-pensioner).
1.2.2.1. Pro: In the UK, the elderly are [wealthier](https://www.theguardian.com/business/2019/dec/08/older-voters-election-2019-assets-tax-labour-tories-social-care) than other age groups. Their preferred taxation policies can depart from the interests of voters in other age groups who would benefit from more redistributive policies.
1.2.2.1.1. Con: The elderly generally have paid more taxes and therefore have should be allowed a greater voice in decisions concerning financial policy.
1.2.2.2. Pro: Political parties have pitted the elderly against younger voters in attempts to gain from [polarization](https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2018/dec/23/old-people-enemies-young-generational-compact).
1.2.2.3. Pro: Voter turnout levels for older people are [consistently higher than for younger people.](http://www.electproject.org/home/voter-turnout/demographics)
1.2.2.4. Con: Politicians such as [Bernie Sanders](https://www.vox.com/2019/3/7/18216899/bernie-sanders-bro-base-polling-2020-president) have primarily campaigned around the interests of the young.
1.2.2.5. Con: The solution to a disparity in voter participation rates among different groups is to take measures that encourage the group with lower participation rates to vote more.
1.2.3. Con: The elderly are [less productive](http://www.nber.org/papers/w22452) and therefore make a relatively lower contribution to the economy. Their interests should also be given less importance than the interests of those in productive age groups.
1.2.3.1. Pro: The [negative contribution](https://doi.org/10.1016/s0140-6736\(14\)61464-1) of the elderly to the economy is also higher because the government is required to spend on assistance and welfare.
1.2.3.1.1. Pro: In the US, it is estimated that the [government will spend over half the federal budget](https://www.forbes.com/sites/howardgleckman/2019/02/01/the-federal-government-will-spend-half-its-budget-on-older-adults-in-ten-years/?sh=2bfcd61c56b6) on older adults in the form of health care and retirement benefits.
1.2.3.2. Con: It is a duty of governments to [take care of people](https://www.cairn-int.info/journal-revue-europeenne-des-sciences-sociales-2017-1-page-127.htm?try_download=1#) who are no longer able to be productive. If the elderly did not have a right to vote, the state would have less incentive to perform this duty.
1.2.3.3. Con: A study in the UK showed that in 2011 the elderly contributed [40 billion pounds](http://www.cpa.org.uk/cpa/policies_on_ageing.html) more than what the state spent on them.
1.2.3.4. Con: Productivity should not be measured only through economic contribution. The elderly contribute insights gained through their cultural and social experience.
1.2.3.5. Con: Even if the elderly do not sufficiently contribute to the economy, the [macroeconomic situation](https://www.imf.org/external/np/g20/pdf/2019/060519a.pdf) of a country is significantly impacted by them. Therefore, their interests and political choices should be taken seriously.
1.2.4. Con: Many people [vote against their social and economic interests.](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/cutting-edge-leadership/201712/why-do-people-vote-against-their-best-interests)
1.2.4.1. Con: In the [UK](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/brexit/2016/11/22/every-generation-votes-in-their-own-interest-but-in-an-ageing-world-thats-a-problem/), evidence suggests that the elderly vote based on their economic interests.
1.2.4.2. Pro: In America, many elderly people [vote against their interests](https://www.lehighvalleylive.com/opinion/2020/09/seniors-for-trump-voting-against-their-own-interests-letter.html).
1.2.4.3. Con: People should have the right to vote for what they perceive to be in their interest, regardless of whether it is actually in their best interests.
1.2.4.4. Con: Interests are self-defined and thus correspond with individual voting behaviour.
1.2.4.5. Pro: Many working-class people [vote against their economic interests.](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/07/31/books/review/the-system-robert-reich-break-em-up-zephyr-teachout.html)
1.2.4.6. Pro: [Resentment](https://vocal.media/theSwamp/why-do-people-vote-against-their-own-interests) can be a driving factor in people voting against their interests.
1.2.5. Con: Not all groups whose [interests](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/voting/#6) are impacted by political class are allowed to participate in elections. Non-citizens, for instance, are not allowed to vote even though they are an interest group.
1.2.6. Pro: Younger generations are not affected as much by things like retirement policy. Elderly people are a constituency with unique interests.
1.2.6.1. Con: Many groups have interests that are specific to members of that group.
1.2.7. Pro: Political parties are less likely to represent the interests of the elderly if the elderly do not make up a part of the electorate.
1.2.7.1. Con: Most non-elderly people hold the view that the [state should support the elderly](http://www.cssr.uct.ac.za/sites/default/files/image_tool/images/256/Publications/WP436Seekings.pdf) through pension and care. There does not seem to be a need for the right to vote to put such welfare systems in place. \(pg. 9\)
1.2.7.2. Pro: If the elderly are not allowed to vote, political parties are less likely to campaign on policy issues that affect the elderly as they would have no say in who is elected and who is not.
1.2.8. Con: There are non-electoral processes and mechanisms through which the needs of the elderly are [represented in policy making](https://academic.oup.com/biomedgerontology/article/57/11/M705/625761).
1.3. Pro: Political views and opinions of the elderly are not relevant to contemporary times and therefore they should not be given a political choice.
1.3.1. Con: Witnessing decades of changes in governmental power, the elder have the wisdom to make an well-informed decision.
1.3.1.1. Con: Past performance is not indicative of future results.
1.3.1.2. Pro: The elderly have a wealth of [life experience](https://www.jrf.org.uk/report/older-peoples-views-and-experiences-resources-later-life), which plays an important role in making important decisions.
1.3.1.3. Con: It is not required to directly experience a historical event in order to learn from it.
1.3.1.4. Pro: The elderly may be better able to predict certain societal outcomes dependent on who is in power, based on their experience.
1.3.1.5. Con: Having wisdom does not mean it will be used wisely. The elderly may do what is within their best interest, instead of looking at the needs of the nation as a whole.
1.3.2. Pro: The elderly are often stubbornly loyal to a political party.
1.3.2.1. Con: Partisan loyalty is not a feature of only the elderly. [Young voters](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2017/sep/23/only-a-quarter-of-older-people-would-consider-voting-labour-poll-shows) also tend to be loyal to a particular party.
1.3.2.2. Con: Older people have seen the politics for longer time, therefore they have more knowledge about the candidates and parties.
1.3.2.3. Con: The elderly in the US have [shifted their voting preference](https://www.washingtonpost.com/outlook/2020/09/28/biden-older-voters-trump-advantage/) from the Republican party to Democratic. This shows that they do not have stubborn partisan loyalty.
1.3.2.4. Pro: The elderly seem to prefer [conservative](https://www.psychologytoday.com/gb/blog/mr-personality/201410/why-are-older-people-more-conservative) parties and policies.
1.3.2.4.1. Pro: The elderly, in the UK, are more overwhelmingly less likely to vote for the [Labour party](https://www.theguardian.com/politics/2017/sep/23/only-a-quarter-of-older-people-would-consider-voting-labour-poll-shows).
1.3.2.4.2. Con: The elderly of our times prefer conservative parties not due to their age but because certain [political events](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2014/07/08/upshot/how-the-year-you-were-born-influences-your-politics.html) were prominent during their youth. This suggests that our experience shapes our political preferences more than age.
1.3.2.4.2.1. Con: Once formed, people's personalities tend to remain stable throughout their lives. Aging does not significantly impact [personality](https://www.vox.com/2016/7/1/12051622/brexit-vote-age-gap-aging-science-psychology).
1.3.2.4.2.1.1. Con: Age plays an important role in the construction of one's [social identity](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/brexit/2016/11/22/every-generation-votes-in-their-own-interest-but-in-an-ageing-world-thats-a-problem/).
1.3.2.4.2.2. Pro: Several studies in the US have shown that those who are currently the elderly formed their political preferences during younger days.
1.3.2.4.2.2.1. Pro: A [study](http://www.stat.columbia.edu/~gelman/research/unpublished/cohort_voting_20140605.pdf) in the US showed the political preferences of those currently old were formed during their younger days. This has been especially true for those who identify as New Deal Democrats, Eisenhower Republicans, Baby Boomer, and Reagan Conservatives.
1.3.2.4.2.2.2. Pro: A [survey](https://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2013/11/21/jfk-torch-bearers-now-vote-more-republican/) showed that Americans who were in their teens during Truman and Eisenhower eras are now more likely to identify as Republicans.
1.3.2.4.2.2.3. Pro: A [survey](https://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2013/11/21/jfk-torch-bearers-now-vote-more-republican/) those who came of age during the Bill Clinton era are more likely to vote Democratic and those who did not.
1.3.2.4.3. Con: And youth seem to [prefer](https://www.washingtonpost.com/politics/2019/01/17/next-generation-voters-are-more-liberal-more-inclusive-believe-government/) liberal policies, which proves nothing about which is better.
1.3.2.4.4. Pro: Older people are more likely to hold views [against financial reforms](https://academic.oup.com/restud/article-abstract/71/1/115/1589476) than younger people.
1.3.2.4.4.1. Con: [Fiscal conservatism](https://jacobinmag.com/2020/06/spain-covid-deaths-nursing-homes-madrid) can hurt the well-being of the elderly when it constricts medical spending. It is unlikely that the elderly will continue to support such measures for long.
1.3.2.4.5. Pro: The elderly are less likely to support [policies concerning the protection of the environment](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/brexit/2016/11/22/every-generation-votes-in-their-own-interest-but-in-an-ageing-world-thats-a-problem/) and the use of sustainable energy.
1.3.2.4.6. Con: Our [political identities](https://www.thecut.co/2017/04/what-shapes-your-political-beliefs-at-18-35-and-50.html) are formed through a complex interaction of several factors, including social traits, genes, and social position, and cannot reliably be reduced to a single factor such as age.
1.3.2.4.7. Pro: In the UK, the elderly are more likely to hold [socially conservative views](https://blogs.lse.ac.uk/politicsandpolicy/generation-wars-over-brexit/) associated with nationalism, Euroscepticism, and immigration.
1.3.2.4.8. Con: Conservative policies and parties are still relevant to contemporary times.
1.3.2.4.8.1. Pro: Many of the [current ruling parties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_ruling_political_parties_by_country) across the world espouse conservative ideologies.
1.3.2.5. Con: The elderly know their own ideology and which party supports it more closely. Voting for that party is not a blind process, limited attention to policy details notwithstanding. Indeed, most voters vote this way regardless. A policy awareness test would dramatically reduce voting eligibility
1.3.2.6. Con: Changing the rules will invite efforts to game the system.
1.3.2.7. Pro: In [Tanzania](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/17531055.2017.1410759), the elderly are less likely to look beyond the incumbent party while younger voters are more inclined towards the opposition parties.
1.3.3. Con: There are no defined sets of opinions relevant to contemporary times as individuals [hold a variety of viewpoints.](https://www.futurity.org/political-polarization-parties-united-states-2260412-2/)
1.3.3.1. Pro: The views of an individual can change across the course of their lives. Therefore it is plausible that many elderly people have adopted views that are modern and relevant to contemporary times.
1.3.3.1.1. Con: Older people are [less likely to change their views](https://www.usnews.com/news/politics/articles/2018-08-17/survey-14-percent-of-adults-have-changed-their-mind-because-of-social-media).
1.3.3.1.1.1. Con: [Surveys](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/uk-politics-45098550) concerning a hypothetical second referendum on Brexit should the elderly have changed their views on political ideas such as Brexit.
1.3.3.1.2. Con: [Views are often based on emotions and group membership](https://greatergood.berkeley.edu/article/item/why_is_it_so_hard_to_change_peoples_minds). This means that it can be difficult to use reason in order to change someone's mind. Thus, the views of the elderly may not change in accordance with the general perspectives of contemporary society.
1.3.3.1.2.1. Con: The argument, that one's views are based on emotion and group affiliation with resulting resistance to change, is one which applies equally to people of all ages, not just the elderly.
1.3.3.1.3. Pro: [14% in the US](https://www.pewresearch.org/fact-tank/2018/08/15/14-of-americans-have-changed-their-mind-about-an-issue-because-of-something-they-saw-on-social-media/) say that they have changed their view on an issue due to social media.
1.3.3.1.3.1. Pro: Older adults are [increasingly active on social media.](https://www.cbs.nl/en-gb/news/2017/52/social-media-on-the-rise-among-seniors)
1.3.3.1.3.2. Con: When considering social media use amongst different age groups in the United States, adults over the age of 65 are the [least active on social networks.](https://www.statista.com/statistics/471370/us-adults-who-use-social-networks-age/)
1.3.3.2. Pro: Diverging voting behaviour between individuals shows that people hold different viewpoints.
1.3.4. Con: The elderly are still a part of modern society. Being older does not automatically mean that they hold views that are outdated.
1.3.4.1.
